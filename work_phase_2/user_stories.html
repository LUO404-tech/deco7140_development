User Stories

Alex, 22, university student

Passionate about gaming and cosplay. Uses online forums daily to connect with niche communities.

1. Can you tell me about your experience connecting with others in your hobby or subculture online?

Mostly on Discord and forums. It’s fun ’cause I meet people with the same interests, but sometimes it’s hard—big groups move too fast and my posts just get ignored.

2. Describe a time when you tried to find information, tutorials, or resources related to your hobby. What challenges did you face?

When I tried making my first cosplay sword, I spent forever jumping between YouTube and blogs. Most tutorials skipped steps or assumed I had pro tools. I had to mix a bunch of guides together, which was super frustrating.

3. Can you walk me through your experience of sharing your own creations? What made it positive or difficult?

I usually post cosplay photos on Reddit or Discord. It feels great when people leave nice comments or ask questions. But sometimes I’ll put weeks into a costume and barely get any likes, which kind of sucks. And one mean comment can really stick with you.

4. What online features or tools would make you feel more connected to your community?

Honestly, I’d love one place with good tutorials, an easy way to ask questions, and maybe an event list for cons or tournaments. Also a space to share stuff without it getting lost in the feed.

5. Think about a time when you felt excluded or unwelcome in a hobby community. What happened, and how could a website make the space feel more inclusive?

I once posted my first cosplay and some people said it looked “cheap.” That really put me off sharing again. A site could help by pushing positive feedback, making beginner-friendly areas, and showing that all skill levels are welcome.

6. How do you usually access your community content, and how does this affect your experience?

Mostly on PC, since I’m gaming there. But I check stuff on my phone too. PC is smooth, but on mobile it’s annoying—uploads are clunky and long threads load slowly. If my internet’s bad, I just give up on videos.

7. If you could design the perfect online space for your hobby or subculture, what would it look like, and what features would it include?

Something simple—no clutter, just a feed, tutorials, and an event calendar. A beginner-friendly section would be cool too, so people don’t feel judged.

Kenji, 19, Anime & Manga Collector

First-year university student, collects manga and limited-edition figurines. Uses Reddit and Discord for discussions but often feels lost among scattered communities. Wants better ways to trade/collect items safely and connect with local anime clubs. Prefers visual-heavy websites (images, fan art galleries).

1. Can you tell me about your experience connecting with others in your hobby or subculture online? What worked well, and what didn’t?

I usually connect through Reddit and Discord. What worked well is that it’s easy to find niche groups and lots of passionate people. What didn’t work is that communities are scattered, info gets buried in endless threads, and it’s hard to make real connections.

2. Describe a time when you tried to find information, tutorials, or resources related to your hobby. What challenges did you face?

I tried finding guides on spotting fake figurines. The challenge was that info was spread across random blogs and forum posts, sometimes outdated or inconsistent, so it was hard to know which source to trust.

3. Can you walk me through your experience of sharing your own creations (e.g., photos, music, crafts)? What made it positive or difficult?

I once shared photos of my manga shelf setup on Reddit. It was positive because people liked the display and gave tips. But it was difficult since the images got lost quickly in the feed, and I worried about negative comments or not getting much feedback.

4. What online features or tools would make you feel more connected to your community?

A safe trading system for figures/manga, local club finder, and a gallery space for fan art or collections would help me feel more connected.

5. Think about a time when you felt excluded or unwelcome in a hobby community. What happened, and how could a website make the space feel more inclusive?

Once I asked a newbie question in a Discord server and got ignored while others chatted. A website could be more inclusive by highlighting beginner-friendly spaces, mentorship options, and clear community guidelines to encourage respect.

6. How do you usually access your community content? How does this affect your experience?

Mostly on my phone. It’s convenient, but long forum threads or image-heavy pages can be hard to read and slow to load.

7. If you could design the perfect online space for your hobby or subculture, what would it look like, and what features would it include?

It would be very visual, with galleries for collections and fan art, a safe marketplace for trading, and local club/event listings. Clear tags and search would make finding info easier, and a supportive community vibe would be key.
Lila, 28, Indie Music Enthusiast

Works as a part-time barista, spends weekends going to underground gigs. Passionate about discovering indie bands and sharing playlists. Active on niche forums and SoundCloud, but struggles to find centralized spaces for small communities. Wants to connect with other fans and support emerging artists.

1. Can you tell me about your experience connecting with others in your hobby or subculture online? What worked well, and what didn’t?

I usually hang out on smaller forums and on SoundCloud, sharing songs and playlists. What worked well was finding people with really niche tastes—it feels special when someone gets excited about a band you just discovered. What didn’t work was that the communities are really scattered. Sometimes I feel like I have to jump between five different sites to stay connected, and conversations fade quickly.

2. Describe a time when you tried to find information, tutorials, or resources related to your hobby. What challenges did you face?

I once tried to find a guide on how to record decent-quality live sets with budget gear, but I mostly found YouTube videos that were too broad or focused on expensive equipment. The challenge was filtering out what was useful for indie/DIY contexts versus pro setups.

3. Can you walk me through your experience of sharing your own creations (e.g., playlists, gig photos)? What made it positive or difficult?

Sharing playlists is fun—I love when people actually follow them or message me about discovering a new band. But it can be discouraging when posts just sink into the feed with no interaction. Sometimes I’m not sure if it’s about the algorithm or just lack of interest.

4. What online features or tools would make you feel more connected to your community?

Definitely event listings for underground shows, plus easier ways to collaborate on playlists or zines, and a space to support emerging artists directly — like tipping, buying merch, or boosting their gigs.

5. Think about a time when you felt excluded or unwelcome in a hobby community. What happened, and how could a website make the space feel more inclusive?

There were times on forums where people acted elitist, dismissing newer fans for not knowing obscure bands. It felt unwelcoming. A website could fix that by highlighting community guidelines, promoting respectful discussion, and maybe having mentorship features where experienced fans help newcomers instead of gatekeeping.

6. How do you usually access your community content? How does this affect your experience?

Mostly on my phone during breaks at work or while commuting. It’s convenient, but sometimes smaller sites don’t have great mobile layouts, which makes it frustrating to engage fully.

7. If you could design the perfect online space for your hobby or subculture, what would it look like, and what features would it include?

It would combine event calendars, fan-made content, and artist support tools in one place. A clean space without too many ads, where I can both chat casually and dive deep into discussions. Collaborative playlists, live chat during gigs, and ways to directly support indie bands would make it feel complete.

Fatima, 31, Knitting & Craft Subculture Member

Works as a primary school teacher, enjoys knitting and DIY crafts as a creative outlet. Shares her work on Instagram, but feels mainstream platforms don’t support detailed tutorials or niche community bonding. Wants collaborative features (patterns exchange, project groups). Values inclusivity and safe spaces where beginners are welcomed.

1. Can you tell me about your experience connecting with others in your hobby or subculture online? What worked well, and what didn’t?

I’ve mostly connected with other knitters and crafters through Instagram and some Facebook groups. What worked well is the inspiration—I can see so many projects and get motivated. But what didn’t work was the lack of depth. Instagram is too visual for detailed tutorials, and in Facebook groups, beginners’ questions sometimes get ignored or conversations go off-topic.

2. Describe a time when you tried to find information, tutorials, or resources related to your hobby. What challenges did you face?

When I tried to learn a lace knitting technique, I had to jump between videos, blogs, and forums. The main challenge was that resources were scattered and inconsistent—some skipped steps, some assumed I already knew advanced terms. It was frustrating because I wanted one clear, step-by-step guide with space to ask questions.

3. Can you walk me through your experience of sharing your own creations (e.g., photos, music, crafts)? What made it positive or difficult?

I usually share on Instagram. The positive side is the encouragement from others, which makes me proud. But it’s hard to post step-by-step tutorials there—they get lost in the feed. Also, I sometimes feel intimidated, wondering if my work is “good enough” compared to more professional makers.

4. What online features or tools would make you feel more connected to your community?

Features for collaboration, like pattern exchanges and project groups, plus a categorized Q&A and beginner-friendly spaces with mentorship would help a lot.

5. Think about a time when you felt excluded or unwelcome in a hobby community. What happened, and how could a website make the space feel more inclusive?

In Facebook groups, I sometimes saw advanced members dismiss beginner questions. That made the space intimidating. A website could fix that with clear community guidelines, beginner-friendly sections, and an inclusive culture that encourages respect.

6. How do you usually access your community content? How does this affect your experience?

I usually browse on my phone because it’s convenient. But the small screen makes it hard to follow detailed tutorials or patterns. On my laptop, it’s easier to focus, but it feels less social than casually scrolling on mobile.

7. If you could design the perfect online space for your hobby or subculture, what would it look like, and what features would it include?

It would be a dedicated craft community with sections by skill level and craft type. There’d be pattern exchanges, project groups, and tutorials you can save. Plus, chat or forum areas for small-group connections, and strong inclusivity guidelines to welcome beginners.

Diego, 24, Street Dance & Urban Culture Enthusiast

Part of a local street dance crew, attends urban culture events (graffiti, freestyle rap). Uses YouTube and TikTok to share performances but struggles with lack of community-focused platforms. Wants event directories for battles/workshops and spaces for collaboration with other artists. Seeks mobile-first design since he’s often accessing content on the go.

1. Can you tell me about your experience connecting with others in your hobby or subculture online? What worked well, and what didn’t?

I usually connect with other dancers through Instagram and TikTok — posting clips, checking out others’ routines, and sometimes joining live sessions. What works well is the quick exposure and finding new styles. But it doesn’t feel like a real community — everything is surface-level, and it’s hard to build deeper connections or organize events just through those platforms.

2. Describe a time when you tried to find information, tutorials, or resources related to your hobby. What challenges did you face?

When I wanted to learn some new popping techniques, I searched on YouTube. The challenge was that tutorials were scattered, with different quality and no clear progression. It took a lot of time to figure out which ones were legit and useful, and there wasn’t one place to find structured resources.

3. Can you walk me through your experience of sharing your own creations (e.g., playlists, gig photos)? What made it positive or difficult?

I often post clips of our crew’s battles on TikTok. The positive part is getting quick feedback and hype from people, sometimes even from dancers abroad. The difficult part is that the platform pushes trends, so niche or experimental stuff doesn’t get much visibility. It feels like you’re chasing algorithms instead of sharing for the community.

4. What online features or tools (e.g., event listings, chat spaces, mentorship, resource libraries) would make you feel more connected to your community?

An event directory for battles, workshops, and jams would be super helpful. Also, a space where dancers, graffiti artists, and MCs can collaborate — like shared project boards or chat groups. A mentorship section, where experienced dancers can guide beginners, would make the community stronger too.

5. Think about a time when you felt excluded or unwelcome in a hobby community. What happened, and how could a website make the space feel more inclusive?

Once at a local jam, the vibe felt cliquish — only certain crews got attention, and newcomers were overlooked. Online, the same thing happens when only “big names” get visibility. A website could fix this by highlighting fresh talent, rotating featured artists, and having spaces where beginners feel safe to share without judgment.

6. How do you usually access your community content (e.g., mobile, PC, slow internet)? How does this affect your experience?

I mostly use my phone, usually on the go between classes and practice. Mobile-first design is key — if a site isn’t optimized, it feels clunky and I just drop it. Quick loading and clean layouts make the experience way smoother.

7. If you could design the perfect online space for your hobby or subculture, what would it look like, and what features would it include?

I’d want a place with an easy event map, spots to collab with other artists, and well-organized tutorials—most importantly, it should highlight newcomers too, not just the big names . Something that feels built for the community, not just trends.
